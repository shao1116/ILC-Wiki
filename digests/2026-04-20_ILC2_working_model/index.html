    ILC2 Working Model

    Scope

    This digest organizes the current ILC_in_lung evidence base around group 2 innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s), with emphasis on lung and airway inflammation, respiratory viral infection, tissue repair, metabolic control, neuroimmune regulation, and plasticity.

    This page is a biological working model for lung and airway ILC2 function. It emphasizes evidence-supported ILC2 claims and preserves species, tissue, and disease-context boundaries.

    Working model

    Lung and airway ILC2s behave less like a single fixed effector cell type and more like a tissue-positioned response module that can swing between type 2 inflammation, epithelial repair, macrophage-niche control, memory-like amplification, and non-type-2 plasticity. Human lung tissue contains identifiable ILC2s, giving the model a direct human pulmonary anchor, while mouse lung perturbation studies provide much of the causal mechanistic evidence.

    Viral lung disease establishes two important anchors. Influenza can activate innate lymphoid cells in a way that drives airway hyperreactivity independently of adaptive TH2 immunity, but lung ILCs can also promote tissue homeostasis after influenza injury through amphiregulin-associated repair. BATF sharpens this repair branch by linking wound-healing-enriched ILC2 identity to protection during acute respiratory viral infection. Gammaherpesvirus conditioning adds a third viral branch in which ILC2s reduce type 2 output while producing GM-CSF that instructs monocyte-derived alveolar macrophage identity.

    In asthma and allergic airway models, ILC2s often amplify disease through IL-5/IL-13, mucus-related pathology, eosinophilia, AHR, IL-33/IL-25-driven accumulation, CXCL16 migration, cysteinyl leukotriene signaling, allergen-experienced memory-like responses, metabolic programs such as autophagy and HIF-1alpha/glycolysis, mTORC1-linked neuroimmune crosstalk, and SCF/c-Kit regulation. These disease-amplifying roles should be separated from repair roles rather than collapsed into a single "ILC2 activation" label.

    The most important update from the high-priority crystallization passes is that ILC2 plasticity is now a central part of the lung model. COPD-associated infectious or noxious triggers can push ILC2s toward an IL-12/IL-18-regulated T-bet/IFN-gamma ILC1-like state. Papain or IL-33 plus leukotrienes can induce pathogenic IL-17-producing ST2+ ILC2s. Human nasal inflammation shows IL-1beta/IL-23/TGF-beta-driven ILC2-to-IL-17-producing plasticity, but that claim should stay labeled as nasal/airway evidence rather than lower-lung proof.

    Highest-confidence claims

    • High confidence: human lung contains identifiable ILC2s, and mouse/human airway evidence supports ILC2s as pulmonary immune cells rather than purely extrapulmonary or theoretical entities.
    • High confidence: ILC2s can drive allergic/type 2 airway pathology and AHR through IL-5/IL-13-associated inflammation, IL-33/IL-25 responsiveness, CXCL16 migration, cysteinyl leukotriene signaling, and allergen-experienced memory-like amplification.
    • High confidence: ILC2s can support post-viral epithelial repair and lung function through amphiregulin-associated repair biology and BATF-linked protective wound-healing identity.
    • High confidence: ILC2s can be reprogrammed by viral or inflammatory contexts, including gammaherpesvirus-conditioned GM-CSF production that imprints monocyte-derived alveolar macrophages and COPD-associated IL-12/IL-18-driven ILC1-like conversion.
    • Medium-high confidence: IL-17-producing ST2+ ILC2s are a pathogenic lung inflammation branch in mouse models, while human nasal ILC2-to-IL-17 plasticity provides airway-relevant but not lower-lung proof.
    • Medium-high confidence: SCF/c-Kit, autophagy, HIF-1alpha/glycolysis, mTORC1, dopamine/mitochondrial activity, and neuroimmune circuits are regulatory nodes for ILC2 function, but their disease specificity varies by model and tissue compartment.

    Claim-level confidence boundaries

    • High confidence is used for ILC2 claims supported by direct lung or airway evidence linking ILC2 identity to cytokine output, repair activity, airway physiology, or macrophage imprinting.
    • Medium-high confidence is used for regulatory or plasticity mechanisms that are biologically coherent and experimentally supported, but whose lower-lung, human, or disease-general relevance needs tighter mapping.
    • Human nasal, sputum, blood, and lung tissue findings should be labeled by compartment; they should not be upgraded to pan-lung causal claims without matched functional evidence.

    Contradictions to track

    • Protective versus pathogenic: ILC2s can support post-viral repair and tissue protection, yet also drive airway hyperreactivity and allergic inflammation. This is a context boundary, not a true supersession.
    • Type 2 versus non-type 2 output: some sources frame ILC2s through IL-5/IL-13 and type 2 inflammation, while others emphasize IL-17-producing ST2+ ILC2s, ILC3-like plasticity, or GM-CSF-dependent macrophage effects.
    • Mouse versus human and compartment: many mechanistic sources are murine, while human lung, sputum, blood, and nasal-polyp sources often provide association, frequency, or ex vivo function. Do not merge these evidence types without labels.
    • Cell number versus function: increased ILC2 abundance, altered cytokine output, and altered tissue repair are related but should be curated as distinct claim types.
    • SCF branch specificity: SCF/c-Kit can regulate ILC2 in chronic allergic disease and ILC3 in neutrophilic asthma-like inflammation, but these are distinct axes until directly compared.

    2026-04-23 Focused Update

    The ILC2 working model now explicitly includes a niche-and-brake layer. Lung ILC2s can be supported by adventitial stromal cells that provide IL-33 and TSLP, while ILC2-derived IL-13 feeds back on that stromal niche. IFN-gamma is now curated as a context-dependent ILC2 brake: it can suppress IL-33-driven activation, limit parenchymal trafficking and survival during mixed inflammation, inhibit ILC2-driven AHR downstream of TLR9/type I IFN/NK-cell signaling, and suppress protective ILC2 IL-5/amphiregulin output during H1N1 influenza.

    This does not replace the prior pathogenic-versus-reparative ILC2 model. It adds a spatial and counter-regulatory layer that helps explain why ILC2 activation can drive allergic disease in one context, support repair in another, and become confined during type 1/type 2 mixed inflammation.
