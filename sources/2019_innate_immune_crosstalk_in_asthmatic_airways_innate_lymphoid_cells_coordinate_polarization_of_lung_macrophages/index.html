    Innate immune crosstalk in asthmatic airways: Innate lymphoid cells coordinate polarization of lung macrophages

    Citation

    • Verified title: Innate immune crosstalk in asthmatic airways: Innate lymphoid cells coordinate polarization of lung macrophages
    • Publication year: 2019
    • DOI: 10.1016/j.jaci.2018.10.040
    • Metadata source: crossref-doi (confidence: high)
    • Original local title: Innate immune crosstalk in asthmatic airways Innate lymphoid cells coordinate polarization of lung macrophages

    Ingest Mode

    • Mode: focused manual crystallization mode
    • Meaning: this source page has been manually upgraded for a priority crystallization pass and may support durable topic/entity/project/digest synthesis.
    • Required boundary: reusable claims from this source still need source-linked species, tissue, assay, model, and confidence labels.

    Source Type

    • primary human asthma induced-sputum and macrophage coculture study
    • ILC subset-macrophage polarization source
    • eosinophilic versus noneosinophilic asthma immune-crosstalk paper
    • crystallization status: manually promoted into batch 2 high-priority lung ILC source note

    Evidence Profile

    • Overall confidence: high for association of asthma sputum ILC subsets with macrophage polarization patterns and ex vivo coculture effects in the source.
    • Directness to this project: high for human airway ILC subset disease phenotyping and macrophage crosstalk.
    • Evidence type: induced sputum from steroid-naive asthma subjects and healthy donors, flow cytometry, alveolar macrophage/ILC subset coculture, cytokine and contact-dependent interpretation.
    • Main biological axis: eosinophilic asthma -> ILC2/M2-like macrophage association; noneosinophilic asthma -> ILC1/ILC3/M1-like macrophage association.

    Why It Matters Here

    This source connects human airway ILC subset balance to macrophage polarization and asthma phenotype. It complements smoke-associated ILC3/M1 findings and helps interpret ILC2 versus ILC1/3 branches in asthma.

    Key Findings

    • ILC1s and ILC3s, in addition to ILC2s, were increased in induced sputum from asthma patients relative to healthy donors.
    • ILC2 numbers correlated with M2 macrophages, while ILC1/ILC3 numbers correlated with M1 macrophages.
    • Coculture of ILC2s with alveolar macrophages induced M2-related genes, whereas ILC1/ILC3 coculture induced M1-related genes through cytokine secretion and cell-cell contact.
    • Eosinophilic asthma showed more ILC2/M2 profile, whereas noneosinophilic asthma showed an M1 macrophage-dominant profile.

    Claim-Level Confidence

    • High confidence: airway ILC subset composition differs by asthma phenotype in this human induced-sputum study.
    • High confidence: ILC2s align with M2-like macrophage polarization, while ILC1/ILC3s align with M1-like macrophage polarization in the reported assays.
    • Medium confidence: ILC-macrophage crosstalk may help structure eosinophilic versus noneosinophilic asthma, but patient-level causality remains incompletely proven.
    • Low confidence: induced sputum findings should not be treated as a full map of lung tissue-resident ILC niches.

    Methods and Context

    • Induced sputum from steroid-naive asthma subjects and healthy donors.
    • Flow cytometry of ILC subsets and macrophage phenotypes.
    • Sorted alveolar macrophage and ILC subset coculture.
    • Best used for airway immune-crosstalk and phenotype association.

    Caveats

    • Sputum cell composition differs from parenchymal lung tissue.
    • Coculture data support functional capacity but do not fully reconstruct the in vivo airway niche.
    • Steroid-naive cohort context should be kept separate from steroid-resistant asthma claims.

    Contradiction and Supersession

    • Contradiction/tension: ILC2-M2 and ILC1/3-M1 branches should be treated as phenotype-linked patterns, not universal asthma rules.
    • Supersession status: not superseded; use as a human airway crosstalk source.
