## Why It Matters Here

This source directly expands the lung ILC3 disease model beyond local lung cues by adding a gut-lung microbiome axis. It should be used for hypersensitivity-pneumonitis/type 3 inflammation logic, not as a general claim that all antibiotics worsen all asthma or lung disease.

## Key Findings

- Low-dose streptomycin dysbiosis primed mouse lungs for stronger Th1/Th17 and ILC3-associated responses during hypersensitivity pneumonitis challenge.
- Lung ILC3s, Th17 cells, and APC activation increased in the reported dysbiotic context.
- IL-23 neutralization, isolithocholic acid treatment, and mTORC1 inhibition reduced type 3 inflammatory readouts and pathology in the reported system.
- Metagenomic analysis linked dysbiosis to reduced microbial diversity, loss of bile-acid-metabolizing bacteria, and enrichment of branched-chain amino-acid biosynthesis pathways.

## Claim-Level Confidence

- High confidence: gut dysbiosis can prime lung ILC3/Th17-associated inflammation in this mouse HP model.
- Medium-high confidence: IL-23, isoLCA/RORgammat-linked bile-acid biology, and mTORC1 are mechanistic intervention points in the reported system.
- Medium confidence: the source supports a gut-lung type 3 inflammatory-axis topic for this wiki.
- Low confidence: the findings should not be generalized to all antibiotic exposures, all microbiome changes, or all pulmonary diseases.

## Caveats

- Preserve the streptomycin, mouse, and HP-model labels.
- Do not merge this with vancomycin/ILC2/allergic-response literature without specifying antibiotic, microbiome shift, and disease model.
- Th17 and ILC3 outputs are coupled in the source; avoid assigning all effects exclusively to ILC3.
