## Why It Matters Here

This source is a high-value bridge between ILC2 repair and ILC plasticity. It explains why lung ILC2 identity maintenance matters during acute viral infection.

## Key Findings

- A transcriptionally distinct lung ILC2 subset enriched for wound-healing genes and BATF was identified during acute respiratory viral infection.
- BATF promoted ILC2 proliferation and function and restricted ILC2 plasticity during influenza infection.
- Loss of BATF caused ILC2s to lose protective properties and acquire pathogenic ILC3-like functions.
- BATF directly bound regulatory elements of wound-healing genes, maintained chromatin accessibility, and participated in an IL-33/ST2 feed-forward loop.

## Claim-Level Confidence

- High confidence:
  BATF supports protective, wound-healing-enriched ILC2 identity during acute respiratory viral infection.
- High confidence:
  BATF loss promotes harmful ILC2 plasticity toward ILC3-like inflammatory functions in this model.
- Medium confidence:
  BATF is a candidate therapeutic target for inflammatory resolution, but intervention strategy and human translation need separate support.
- Low confidence:
  this source should not be used to claim all ILC2-to-ILC3-like plasticity is BATF-dependent.

## Methods and Context

- Acute influenza virus infection model.
- Single-cell RNA-seq and regulatory/chromatin analyses.
- BATF perturbation used to test identity, proliferation, and function.
- Readouts include neutrophil infiltration, tissue damage, respiratory failure, and wound-healing gene expression.

## Caveats

- Strongest evidence is in acute viral infection, not chronic asthma.
- BATF effects may depend on infection timing and tissue state.
- Human translation remains to be validated.

## Contradiction and Supersession

- Contradiction/tension: integrates repair and pathology by showing that ILC2 identity maintenance can prevent ILC3-like harmful functions.
- Supersession status: not superseded; it refines earlier influenza ILC repair/pathology papers mechanistically.
