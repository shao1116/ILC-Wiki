## Why It Matters Here

This paper prevents an overly static lung ILC2 model by showing that lung ILC2s can participate in an inter-organ maturation/trafficking axis.

## Key Findings

- Lung-resident ILC2s express CCR2, whereas intestinal ILC2s express CCR4 in the reported mouse system.
- IL-33 induces translocation of ILC2s along a lung-gut axis, and lung-origin ILC2s can contribute to intestinal ILC2 maturation.
- The lung-gut ILC2 trajectory is observed during postnatal development and can be affected by allergen-induced lung ILC2 activation.
- This supports a developmental and inter-organ layer of ILC2 specialization rather than a purely local lung-only model.

## Claim-Level Confidence

- High confidence: CCR2/CCR4 distinguish lung and intestinal ILC2 states in the reported mouse system.
- Medium-high confidence: IL-33-driven lung-gut ILC2 movement is source-supported, but human conservation remains unresolved.
- Medium confidence: HDM-linked effects on gut ILC2 homeostasis should remain labeled as mouse inter-organ evidence.

## Methods and Context

- Species/context: mouse lung and small intestine, including postnatal and allergic airway perturbation contexts.
- Assay directness: reporter, transfer/reconstitution, and tissue profiling evidence.
- Best wiki use: ILC2 tissue specialization, CCR2/CCR4 markers, and lung-gut axis.

## Caveats

- Do not infer that human lung ILC2s routinely traffic to gut without direct human evidence.
- Keep maturation, migration, and activation as separate claims.
