## Why It Matters Here

This source is useful because it shows that an airway particulate/allergen-like stimulus can activate parallel inflammatory modules: an ILC2 type 2/eosinophil/AAM branch and a gamma delta T/IL-17A/neutrophil branch.

## Key Findings

- Chitin induces epithelial IL-25, IL-33, and TSLP and activates resident lung ILC2s to produce IL-5 and IL-13.
- ILC2 loss abrogates eosinophil and alternatively activated macrophage accumulation after chitin exposure.
- Loss of IL-25, IL-33, and TSLP signaling together impairs ILC2 cytokine function, indicating redundant or cooperating epithelial alarmin inputs.
- ILC2 deficiency enhances IL-1beta/TNF/IL-17A-associated gamma delta T-cell and neutrophilic inflammation, supporting a cross-regulatory relationship between type 2 and IL-17/neutrophil modules.

## Claim-Level Confidence

- High confidence: in the chitin mouse model, ILC2s are required for the eosinophil/AAM type 2 branch.
- High confidence: epithelial IL-25, IL-33, and TSLP cooperate to support chitin-induced ILC2 function.
- Medium-high confidence: the ILC2 restraint of neutrophilic/gamma delta T inflammation is model-specific but important for mixed inflammation interpretation.

## Methods and Context

- Species/context: mouse lung exposed to inhaled chitin.
- Assay directness: direct ILC2 perturbation and inflammatory-output measurements.
- Best wiki use: airway alarmin redundancy, ILC2-driven eosinophil/AAM biology, and type 2 versus IL-17/neutrophil cross-regulation.

## Caveats

- Chitin is a model stimulus; do not equate it with all allergens or all fungal/parasite exposures.
- Gamma delta T-cell findings should not be relabeled as ILC3 biology, although they are relevant to IL-17/neutrophil modules.
